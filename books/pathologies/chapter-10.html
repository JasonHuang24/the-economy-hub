Who profits from selling you a future?

A family of legal businesses — multi-level marketing, for-profit colleges, coding bootcamps and their income-share agreements, franchise systems — runs one revenue model. The product is the buyer’s own projected future income, and the seller controls the disclosure that sets the projection. The frauds of an earlier chapter were priced by enforcement; these are priced by disclosure, and they survive by shaping the rules that define them.

In this chapter

The packet runs to forty pages, and the page that matters is near the back. In front are the photographs: the founder on a stage, the couple who paid off the house, the car with the company crest on the grille, the flip-chart arithmetic that turns a few hours a week into a second income and then a first. The page near the back is plainer. It is there because a regulator, at some point, required it, and it reports what the people already in the business actually earned last year. Most of them earned almost nothing. A share the document states in a footnote earned less than the cost of the product they had to buy to take part. The two halves of the packet do not contradict each other. The photographs are true; those people exist. The table is true; that distribution is real. What the buyer is being sold is the distance between them, and the right to believe they will land at the front of the book rather than the back.

The same packet, in different clothes, is handed across a desk at a for-profit college, quoted in a coding bootcamp’s enrollment agreement, and bound into the disclosure document a franchise hands a prospect. In each case the thing being sold is not really a product or a course or a storefront. It is a claim on the buyer’s own future earnings: a projection of what the buyer will make once they have paid to join. And in each case the seller is the party that assembles, frames, and to a large degree controls the information the buyer uses to judge that projection. This chapter is about that arrangement, which is legal, common, and older than any of the particular industries that practice it.

The product is a probability distribution

Strip the settings away and the businesses look alike. A buyer pays a cost now — a starter kit, a tuition bill, a franchise fee — against a return that is not a fixed sum but a spread of possible outcomes, most of them low and a few of them high. What the buyer is really purchasing is a position in that spread, a draw from a distribution of future incomes. The seller cannot change the buyer’s draw. What the seller controls is the buyer’s estimate of it: which outcomes get shown, whether the number on the page is a median or an average, whether the people in the photographs are typical or exceptional, and whether the flat truth about the distribution appears at all, or appears only where a rule forces it and hope will not look.

This is the legal mirror of the businesses treated earlier in this volume. A scam, that chapter argued, is a business whose expected cost, the odds of being caught times the penalty, sits below its expected take; it is priced by enforcement, and it lives in the lag before the law catches up. The businesses in this chapter are not that. They are lawful, and they intend to stay lawful, because their advantage is not a head start on the police but a hand on the disclosure. The relevant law here is disclosure: the body of rules that settles what the seller must tell the buyer before the sale, and how that telling is framed. The criminal code that punishes a lie after the money is gone barely enters. Where the earlier chapter’s operator evaded the rules, this one writes to them, and often helps write them.

The line the industry drew

Multi-level marketing is the oldest and purest case, and it owes its legality to a single decision. In 1979 the Federal Trade Commission investigated Amway and declined to call it an illegal pyramid scheme. A pyramid, under the test the Commission had set out earlier in the Koscot case, pays chiefly for recruiting new participants rather than for selling product to real customers, and collapses when recruitment runs out. Amway, the Commission found, was different, because its rules tied a distributor’s bonuses to actual retail sales. Three rules did the work. The seventy-percent rule required a distributor to sell at least 70 percent of the products bought in a month before collecting a bonus. The ten-customer rule required at least one retail sale to each of ten different customers a month. The buy-back rule required a sponsor to repurchase a departing recruit’s unsold, marketable inventory, removing the incentive to load newcomers with goods they could never move. Those safeguards, the FTC held, kept the plan honest enough to be lawful.

The decision did two things at once, and both matter here. It legalized the model, and it drew the line every multi-level company has policed ever since: recruit-driven and you are a pyramid and illegal, retail-anchored and you are a marketing plan and legal. And in the same order the Commission told Amway to stop misrepresenting how much money distributors were likely to make, which is the disclosure problem this chapter turns on, present at the creation of the legal form. The line has held for more than forty years. It is a real distinction, and it is also one the industry helped fix in place, because on the retail-anchored side of it a business can show recruits the incomes at the top of the plan while the disclosure, printed because that same case required it, reports the distribution near the bottom. The numbers in the first figure are what that disclosure says when it is read: an average dragged upward by a thin top tier, a median far below it, and a typical participant who, after paying for the product, is at or under break-even.

The pipeline

For-profit colleges sell the same projection through a different pipe, and the pipe is federal. The product is a credential and the promise attached to it is future earnings; the money that pays for it arrives, overwhelmingly, as federal student loans and grants under Title IV of the Higher Education Act. That funding is the industry’s lifeline and its one hard constraint. Since 1992 the law has capped how much of a proprietary school’s revenue may come from federal student aid: the original rule required 15 percent from other sources, the “85/15” rule, and in 1998 Congress loosened it to 90/10, letting a school draw up to 90 percent of revenue from Title IV. For years the cap had a gap in it. Veterans’ GI Bill benefits and the Defense Department’s tuition assistance were federal money that did not count as Title IV, so they sat on the ten-percent side of the ratio, and every enrolled veteran let a school take on roughly nine more students paying with federal loans while still satisfying the rule. That arithmetic turned service members into recruiting targets until the American Rescue Plan closed the gap in 2021, with the Department of Education’s implementing rule taking effect in 2023.

Where the money enters, the disclosure fight follows, and its name is gainful employment. The idea is a disclosure-and-accountability metric: measure a program’s graduates against their debt, and, in the current version, against what a worker with only a high-school diploma earns, and cut off federal aid to programs whose graduates cannot clear the bar. It is the exact number the first figure turns on, applied by the government as a condition of the subsidy. The reason it makes a useful case study is not that it works but that it will not stay dead. The rule was issued in 2011, struck down in part by a federal court in 2012, rewritten and reissued in 2014, rescinded outright in 2019, and revived again in 2023. The metric barely moved across those versions. What moved was which administration held the pen and how hard the sector pushed. The second figure lays the sequence out.

The buyers on this side of the family are not drawn at random from the population. For-profit enrollment skews toward older, lower-income, and minority students, the same households that pay more for the same goods everywhere else, and the debt that funds a weak credential is one more charge added to a life in which it is already expensive to be poor. A credential that does not raise earnings is not a neutral disappointment for a student who borrowed to buy it. It is a transfer from the borrower, and through the federal loan program the taxpayer, to the school, secured against a future that the school’s own recruiting described and the disclosure could not correct.

The newest skin

The income-share agreement is the mechanism wearing its most modern clothes. A coding bootcamp offers to train a student for a software job and, instead of charging tuition up front, takes a fixed share of the student’s income for a set number of months once earnings pass a threshold. The pitch is that incentives are finally aligned: the school is paid only if the student is paid, so the school must have faith in its own placement rate. The structure is genuinely different from a loan in who bears the downside. What does not change is who controls the number. The placement rate and the starting salary that make the agreement look like a good trade are compiled, defined, and published by the school, and the student signs against the school’s account of a future the school is selling.

The clearest record comes from the enforcement file. In 2024 the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau found that BloomTech, the coding school formerly called Lambda School, and its chief executive had advertised job-placement rates as high as 86 percent while the school’s own internal figures put placement near 50 percent, and in some groups as low as 30. The same order found the school had told students its income-share agreement was “not a loan” and carried no finance charge, when in fact the agreements averaged a finance charge of about $4,000 and a single missed payment could make the whole $30,000 cap come due. The regulator’s underlying legal point had already been made in 2021, when the Bureau found against another provider, Better Future Forward, and California’s financial regulator settled with the servicer Meratas: an income-share agreement is credit, a loan by another name, and subject to the same disclosure law as any other loan. The instrument was new; the finding was that the old rules applied to it.

The milder cousin

Franchising belongs in the same family by mechanism, and it is important to be exact about where the resemblance stops. The FTC’s Franchise Rule requires a franchisor to give every prospect a long disclosure document, twenty-three numbered items covering fees, obligations, litigation, and the rest. Item 19, the financial performance representation, is the number a buyer most wants: how much a franchisee can expect to earn. It is optional. A franchisor may make an earnings claim, in which case it must have a reasonable basis and written substantiation, or it may make none, in which case the document carries a required sentence stating that the company makes no representation about a franchisee’s future financial performance. The single most decision-relevant figure is left, by the design of the rule, to the seller’s discretion. That is the same disclosure-control this chapter has been tracing.

The mechanism is shared; the severity is not, and the honest comparison has to say so. Franchises fail, sometimes badly, but nothing in the data puts their loss rates near the multi-level figures. When the Government Accountability Office examined one franchise brand’s Small Business Administration loans, it found 74 of 170 had defaulted, though that brand was an outlier concentrated in a few high-volume lenders; typical franchise loan-default rates run in the single digits to the mid-teens. That is far milder than a business model in which the disclosed distribution puts the typical participant below break-even, and the comparison is of size rather than of like for like, since a loan default and a participant’s net loss are not the same event. A franchisee buys a going concern with a known brand, a territory, and, whatever Item 19 omits, a product that customers already want. The claim here is about a common mechanism, control of the projection the buyer is sold, not a common fate. The franchise sits at the mild end of the family precisely because the thing being sold is more than the projection.

Why disclosure keeps losing to hope

Across all four, the chosen remedy is the same, and so is the reason it underperforms. The remedy is disclosure: the income statement in the packet, the gainful-employment metric, Item 19, the finance charge on the income-share agreement. Disclosure is the instrument a system reaches for when it wants to permit a business and protect its customers at the same time, and it fails, reliably, for reasons that have nothing to do with whether the numbers are printed. The seller frames them: an average stands in for a median, so a thin top tier lifts the figure a typical buyer will never see, and the phrase “results not typical” does the rest. The buyer self-selects into the tail: the photographs are of real people, the reader concludes that they are one of them, and a distribution that is true in the aggregate loses to a story that might be true for me. And when disclosure starts to bite, the rule bends, as the second figure shows it bending. A prohibition ends a business; a disclosure only informs one, and information is a weak solvent for hope.

Multi-level marketing is an illegal pyramid scheme.

Oversimplified Moderate confidence

The instinct behind the claim is sound, which is why the ruling is not that its opposite holds. The outcome people picture when they say “pyramid” is real and documented: most participants make little or lose money. Amway’s own 2024 disclosure reports an average of about $723 a year across all its United States distributors, before the cost of the product, and the Federal Trade Commission found half of Herbalife’s incentivized sales leaders earned under $5 a month. Anyone who answers that the typical distributor does well is contradicted by the companies’ own statements. What the claim gets wrong is the word “illegal.” Since the FTC’s 1979 Amway decision, the law has drawn a line between a pyramid, which pays mainly for recruiting, and a multi-level plan, which is anchored to retail sales, and by that line most established multi-level companies are lawful and intend to stay so. The distinction is genuine, and it is also one the industry helped fix in place and polices carefully, because staying on the legal side of it is what lets the business keep selling a projection its own disclosure contradicts. The failure the claim is reaching for is not that these firms are secret crimes. It is that a legal business can sell the buyer a claim on the buyer’s own future income while controlling the information that prices it, and that the only check on the model is a disclosure that hope reads past. That is a milder charge than “illegal pyramid” and a more durable one, because it survives the model being entirely within the law. The claim is oversimplified, right about the losses and wrong, or at least legally beside the point, about the crime.

What the ledger records

Return to the packet, and to the page near the back. Nothing about it was hidden. The distribution was printed, the footnote was accurate, the average was disclosed exactly as a rule required, and none of it changed the sale, because the business was never selling the page. It was selling the photographs, and the buyer’s willingness to place themselves at the front of the book. That is the common thread through multi-level marketing, the for-profit college, the bootcamp with its income-share agreement, and, more gently, the franchise: each sells a projection of the buyer’s own future income, and each controls the information that sets the projection, within a body of disclosure law it helps to shape. The losses are legal. The pathology is not that a crime goes unpunished but that a lawful business can price the distance between what a buyer is shown and what a buyer will get, and can keep pricing it as long as the correcting information is merely required to be present and never required to be believed.
