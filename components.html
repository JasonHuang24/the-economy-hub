How can a country with full granaries suffer famine?

Body text runs at 19px on a 70ch measure. In 1943 Bengal, rice prices roughly quadrupled while agricultural wages moved a few percent; the exchange rate between labor and food collapsed with grain in store.

Kicker: mono, letterspaced, brass-deep in components

Callouts

Three registers. Ledger carries the record, Model carries the theory, Voice carries a quotation.

Ledger

Bengal, 1943, by the numbers

Rice availability in 1943 ran about 5% below the 1941 figure, a normal fluctuation. The price of coarse rice in Calcutta rose from roughly Rs 14 to over Rs 50 per maund within the year while rural wages stayed nearly flat.

Illustrative figures after Sen (1981). Demo content.

Model

The cost-disease mechanism

Let one sector double its output per hour each generation while another, by its nature, holds output per hour fixed. Wages are set economy-wide, so the stagnant sector’s costs rise with the productive sector’s wages. A string quartet takes the same 4 player-hours it took in 1826; the wage those hours must pay has grown a hundredfold.

After Baumol & Bowen (1966). Demo content.

Verdict block

Four levels (Confirmed, Oversimplified, False, Backwards), three confidence tiers (high, moderate, low), and an expandable source footer. Backwards keeps oxide but inverts the chip so the two negative levels read apart.

Services get pricier while manufactured goods get cheaper, across rich countries and decades.

Confirmed High confidence

The relative-price drift shows up in every long consumer price series: education, healthcare, and live performance rise against the basket while electronics, apparel, and appliances fall. The pattern survives changes of country, currency, and index method.

Sources
  • BLS CPI detailed expenditure series, 1980–2025 — demo citation
  • Baumol & Bowen, Performing Arts: The Economic Dilemma (1966) — demo citation

Healthcare is expensive because of administrative waste.

Oversimplified Moderate confidence

Administration adds real cost, and the American share is unusually large. But the underlying drift appears in countries with lean single-payer administration and in sectors with no insurers at all, which points at the labor-cost mechanism rather than paperwork alone.

Sources
  • Cross-country hospital cost decompositions — demo citation
  • OECD relative service price panels — demo citation

Famines happen only when there is not enough food to go around.

False High confidence

Bengal in 1943 held near-normal rice stocks while some three million people died. What collapsed was the exchange rate between a laborer’s wage and grain, a failure of entitlement rather than of supply. Several major famines since fit the same pattern.

Sources
  • Sen, Poverty and Famines (1981) — demo citation
  • Famine Inquiry Commission report (1945) — demo citation

Service prices rise because service workers keep getting less productive.

Backwards Low confidence

The causality runs the other way around. Service productivity mostly stands still; what moves is productivity elsewhere, which bids up the economy-wide wage that service hours must pay. The barber is no slower than in 1950. The factory next door got fast.

Sources
  • Sectoral productivity growth accounting — demo citation
